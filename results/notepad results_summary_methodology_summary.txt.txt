KEY FINDINGS AND IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

Finding 1: Heuristic Methodology is Scalable and Robust
Successfully applied across three planning periods (2024?2030?2035?2040) with 
consistent workflow. Each iteration required 1-3 capacity addition cycles to 
achieve adequacy. Methodology accommodates both generation and transmission 
constraints.

Finding 2: Winter Adequacy is the Binding Constraint
Full annual validation revealed winter months (May-September) drive capacity 
requirements. Summer surplus capacity is necessary to ensure winter adequacy. 
This seasonal pattern will persist in future scenarios and must be considered 
in hydrogen planning.

Finding 3: Strategic Generation Placement Relieves Transmission Congestion
Placing generation near demand centers (Auckland, Huntly) simultaneously 
solves adequacy AND reduces transmission stress. 2030 work showed 93% 
reduction in overloaded lines. This suggests distributed generation may be 
more cost-effective than transmission expansion.

Publication Potential: Standalone paper for NZ electricity sector.

Finding 4: Transmission Becomes Limiting Factor After 2035
Pattern shift observed:
- 2024?2030: Generation bottleneck (added 2,950 MW)
- 2030?2035: Both generation and transmission (added 3,800 MW + 3 lines)
- 2035?2040: Transmission only (0 MW generation, 5 line upgrades)

This suggests generation capacity built in earlier periods can serve future 
demand if transmission is adequate. Implications for long-term infrastructure 
planning and hydrogen scenarios.

Finding 5: Capacity Factor Effect Dominates Technology Mix
Variable renewables require ~3× installed capacity vs. firm generation for 
equivalent effective capacity. Gas-heavy results (5,000 MW cumulative) reflect 
this physics, not policy preference. Hydrogen scenarios will need to address 
this if replacing gas with variable renewables.

Finding 6: Some Corridors Require Multiple Sequential Upgrades
Line N-L82 (Huntly-Stratford) upgraded twice:
- 2035: 600 ? 1000 MVA
- 2040: 1000 ? 1500 MVA

This indicates sustained demand growth in specific corridors. Planning must 
anticipate multi-stage upgrades for critical transmission paths.

Finding 7: Model Validation Requires Careful Tool Understanding
The "stress test" confusion (0.234% persisting despite upgrades) highlighted 
the importance of understanding validation tool behavior. The tool was testing 
future demand, not current year demand. Direct optimization at base year 
demand resolved confusion.

Lesson: Validate your validation tools. Document their behavior thoroughly 
for future users.

Finding 8: Cross-Platform Compatibility Requires Discipline
Unicode character bugs caused Windows/Linux incompatibilities. All scripts 
must use ASCII-only characters. This is critical for reproducibility and 
collaboration.

================================================================================
METHODOLOGY LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

1. Deterministic (No Uncertainty Quantification)
   Single-point estimates for costs, demand growth, technology performance.
   Next phase: Monte Carlo uncertainty analysis addresses this.

2. Simplified Transmission Model
   DC power flow approximation.
   No detailed stability analysis.
   No N-1 security constraints explicitly modeled.

3. No Intertemporal Optimization
   Each planning period optimized independently.
   Does not anticipate future needs when making current decisions.
   Sequential optimization chosen deliberately for consistency with uncertainty 
   analysis philosophy.

4. Limited Operational Detail
   No unit commitment constraints.
   No ramping limits.
   No frequency regulation services.

5. Manual Iteration Required
   Human in the loop for each capacity addition decision.
   Not fully automated (but this is also a strength for policy analysis).

6. Conservative Assumptions May Overstate Requirements
   No emergency reserves modeled.
   No demand response modeled.
   Results represent upper bound on capacity needs.

Workflow Pattern:

Common Pitfalls:
1. Validation tool "stress test" applies scaling ON TOP of scaled demand
2. Generator counts must be validated after network operations
3. Transmission upgrades may need multiple iterations
4. Some lines may require upgrades in multiple planning periods
5. Unicode characters break Windows compatibility

Best Practices:
1. Always validate demand scaling calculations
2. Check generator counts after copying networks
3. Run transmission analysis before declaring adequacy
4. Save results from every iteration for documentation
5. Use descriptive generator names (e.g., NEW2035_gas_OTA_600)
6. Test scripts on both Windows and Linux
7. Document assumptions and rationale in code comments
